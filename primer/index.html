    Part 1. A primer on air pollution, epidemiology, and health¶

    1.1 What air pollution is¶

    Air pollution is a mixture, not a single thing. When we say "air pollution" in a health context, we are usually talking about a handful of regulated pollutants that decades of research have tied to disease. The ones we care about most are fine particulate matter (PM2.5), ground-level ozone (O3), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), black carbon (a component of PM2.5), sulfur dioxide (SO2), and carbon monoxide (CO).

    It helps to separate pollutants into two groups by how they arise:

    Primary pollutants are emitted directly from a source. Black carbon coming out of a diesel tailpipe, SO2 from a coal plant, and primary particles from a cement kiln are all primary.

    Secondary pollutants form in the atmosphere through chemical reactions. Ground-level ozone is the classic example: it is not emitted directly but is produced when nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds react in sunlight. A large share of PM2.5 is also secondary, formed when gases like SO2, NOx, and ammonia convert into sulfate, nitrate, and ammonium particles. This distinction matters for policy, because reducing a secondary pollutant means controlling its gaseous precursors, sometimes ones emitted far away.

    Why PM2.5 matters most for health. Particles in the fine fraction are small enough to bypass the body's upper-airway defenses, travel deep into the gas-exchange regions of the lung, and in the case of the smallest particles, cross into the bloodstream. Once there, they drive inflammation and oxidative stress that affect not just the lungs but the heart and blood vessels, which is why cardiovascular disease, not respiratory disease, accounts for the largest share of PM2.5 deaths. PM2.5 is also a convenient single metric because it is measurable, modelable, and supported by a deep body of epidemiology, which is why most global health burden estimates are built around it.

    Black carbon matters for both our health and climate work. It is a fraction of PM2.5, so it carries the health risks of fine particles, and it is also one of the strongest short-lived warming agents in the atmosphere because it absorbs sunlight directly and darkens snow and ice. Cutting black carbon therefore buys a health benefit and a near-term climate benefit at the same time, which is the premise of the Monterrey project.

    1.2 How air pollution harms health¶

    Inhaled fine particles and reactive gases trigger local inflammation in the airways and lungs. For susceptible people this can mean asthma attacks, worsened chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and respiratory infections. But the more consequential effects are systemic. Particle-driven inflammation and autonomic and vascular changes raise the risk of heart attacks, strokes, high blood pressure, and abnormal heart rhythms over the long term. PM2.5 is an established cause of lung cancer. Growing evidence links air pollution to adverse birth outcomes, diabetes, and neurological effects, though those literatures are less mature.

    When we quantify deaths attributable to PM2.5, the standard set of outcomes (the ones with the strongest causal evidence) are ischemic heart disease, stroke, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, lung cancer, lower respiratory infections, and, more recently, type 2 diabetes. For ozone, the principal outcome is chronic respiratory mortality. For NO2, a major outcome of interest is new childhood asthma.

    Short-term (acute) exposure, a few days of high pollution, can trigger immediate events like hospital admissions and deaths in vulnerable people. Long-term (chronic) exposure, living for years in polluted air, shortens life expectancy across the whole population and accounts for the bulk of the global health burden. Most of our work focuses on chronic effects, but the Monterrey project will also examine short-term links between black carbon and hospital admissions.

    A key finding of modern air pollution epidemiology is that health risks continue down to very low concentrations. There is no level below which PM2.5 is demonstrably harmless. This is why the World Health Organization's 2021 guidelines set the recommended annual PM2.5 level at 5 micrograms per cubic meter, far below where most of the world's population lives, and why reductions are beneficial even in already-clean places.

    1.3 How we know this: a short tour of environmental epidemiology¶

    Everything above rests on epidemiology, the study of how exposures relate to health outcomes in populations. You do not need to become an epidemiologist to do this work, but you should understand the kinds of evidence and their limits.

    Cohort studies follow large groups of people over years, comparing health outcomes across individuals with different long-term exposures. The landmark examples in this field, the Harvard Six Cities study and the American Cancer Society cohort, established that people living in more polluted cities died earlier, chiefly from cardiovascular causes, after accounting for smoking and other factors. Cohort studies are the basis of the long-term concentration-response functions we use.

    Time-series and case-crossover studies relate day-to-day fluctuations in pollution to day-to-day counts of deaths or hospital visits in a city. These are the standard design for short-term effects and are exactly what the Monterrey team will use to link black carbon to respiratory and cardiovascular admissions.

    Key concepts:

    • Confounding. Something that is associated with both the exposure and the outcome, like smoking or income, can create a spurious link. Good studies measure and adjust for confounders.
    • Relative risk and the concentration-response function. Epidemiology gives us a relationship between exposure and the multiplicative increase in risk. Stitched together across the exposure range, this becomes the concentration-response function (CRF) that drives a health impact assessment.
    • Association versus causation. A single study shows association. Causation is inferred from the whole body of evidence: consistency across studies and settings, a dose-response gradient, biological plausibility, and so on. For PM2.5 and cardiovascular and respiratory mortality, that bar has been met, which is why major bodies treat it as causal.

    1.4 The scale of the problem¶

    Air pollution is among the largest environmental risk factors for death worldwide. The Global Burden of Disease study attributes millions of deaths each year to ambient (outdoor) PM2.5, and the broader category of pollution (air, water, lead, and occupational) was estimated to be responsible for roughly 9 million deaths per year, about one in six deaths globally, in the Lancet pollution-and-health updates (Landrigan and colleagues 2018; Fuller and colleagues 2022).

    The burden is very unequal. The large majority of pollution-related deaths occur in low- and middle-income countries, which is why our program focuses on Latin America rather than on places that already have clean air and strong monitoring.

    Cities concentrate the exposure. In the global urban analysis I led (Southerland and colleagues 2022), we estimated that most of the world's urban population lived in areas exceeding the older WHO guideline, and that urban PM2.5 was associated on the order of 1.8 million excess deaths in 2019 alone. A companion analysis tied roughly 2 million new pediatric asthma cases per year to NO2, with most occurring in urban areas (Anenberg and colleagues 2022).

    The fossil fuel share is large. Work using the GEOS-Chem model attributed on the order of 8.7 million deaths per year to PM2.5 from fossil fuel combustion specifically (Vohra and colleagues 2021), which shows how closely the air pollution and climate problems are linked.

    1.5 Greenhouse gases, air pollution, and health: how they connect¶

    1. Shared sources. Burning fossil fuels emits both carbon dioxide (the dominant long-lived greenhouse gas) and health-damaging air pollutants (PM2.5, NOx, SO2, and the precursors of ozone). A coal plant, a diesel truck, or a flaring oil well is simultaneously a climate problem and an air quality problem. This is why cutting fossil fuel use produces a "co-benefit": less warming and cleaner air from the same action.

    2. Short-lived climate pollutants (SLCPs). Some agents both warm the planet and harm health, and they clear from the atmosphere quickly, within days to about a decade, rather than persisting for centuries like CO2. The most important are:

    3. Black carbon, a component of PM2.5 that absorbs sunlight and damages the lungs and heart.
    4. Methane, a potent greenhouse gas that is also the main driver of rising background tropospheric ozone, itself a respiratory hazard and a crop damager.

    5. Tropospheric ozone, both a greenhouse gas and a health hazard. Because SLCPs are short-lived, cutting them slows warming on a timescale that matters for this century, and because they are health-damaging, cutting them saves lives quickly.

    6. Methane is different because its health effect is indirect. It is not itself very toxic to breathe at ambient levels, but it drives the formation of ozone across whole regions and hemispheres. So reducing oil-and-gas methane, the focus of our LIMPIO project, yields a climate benefit (less warming) and a health benefit (less ozone-related mortality), even though no one is "breathing the methane."

    The foundational papers here are worth reading directly. Shindell and colleagues (2012) identified a small set of methane and black carbon control measures that could meaningfully slow near-term warming while preventing on the order of millions of premature deaths and protecting crop yields. Anenberg and colleagues (2012) quantified the global air quality and health co-benefits of those same measures. The 2011 UNEP and WMO Integrated Assessment of Black Carbon and Tropospheric Ozone, which Shindell chaired, helped launch the Climate and Clean Air Coalition. If you understand those three, you understand the thinking behind our whole program.

    The co-benefits story is genuinely strong, but it is not free of debate. Net climate effects of some interventions can be complex (for instance, sources of black carbon often co-emit cooling aerosols, so the warming benefit of a specific control depends on the source mix), and health benefits depend on where reductions happen relative to where people live.

    1.6 Reading List¶

    Foundations and synthesis

    • Pope CA III and Dockery DW. "Health effects of fine particulate air pollution: lines that connect." Journal of the Air and Waste Management Association, 2006. The classic narrative review tying the particulate evidence together. (Start here for the "why does this hurt us" story.)
    • Brook RD and colleagues. "Particulate matter air pollution and cardiovascular disease: an update to the scientific statement from the American Heart Association." Circulation, 2010. The authoritative cardiovascular synthesis.
    • Landrigan PJ and colleagues. "The Lancet Commission on pollution and health." The Lancet, 2018. The big-picture framing of pollution as a global health and equity problem.
    • Fuller R and colleagues. "Pollution and health: a progress update." The Lancet Planetary Health, 2022. (Open access. A copy is also in our shared Drive.)

    Our own work (start here, these use the methods you will learn)

    • Southerland VA and colleagues. "Global urban temporal trends in fine particulate matter (PM2.5) and attributable health burdens: estimates from global datasets." The Lancet Planetary Health, 2022;6(2):e139-e146. (Open access.)
    • Anenberg SC and colleagues. "Long-term trends in urban NO2 concentrations and associated pediatric asthma incidence: estimates from global datasets." The Lancet Planetary Health, 2022. (Open access. The NO2 and childhood asthma companion to the paper above.)

    Greenhouse gases, short-lived climate pollutants, and co-benefits

    • Shindell D and colleagues. "Simultaneously mitigating near-term climate change and improving human health and food security." Science, 2012;335(6065):183-189.
    • Anenberg SC and colleagues. "Global air quality and health co-benefits of mitigating near-term climate change through methane and black carbon emission controls." Environmental Health Perspectives, 2012;120(6):831-839. (Open access.)
    • Bond TC and colleagues. "Bounding the role of black carbon in the climate system: a scientific assessment." Journal of Geophysical Research: Atmospheres, 2013;118(11):5380-5552. (Long, but the definitive black carbon reference. Read the abstract and skim.)
    • Vohra K and colleagues. "Global mortality from outdoor fine particle pollution generated by fossil fuel combustion: results from GEOS-Chem." Environmental Research, 2021;195:110754.

    Authoritative resources and data

    • World Health Organization. "WHO global air quality guidelines: particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), ozone, nitrogen dioxide, sulfur dioxide and carbon monoxide." 2021.
    • Health Effects Institute. State of Global Air. (Excellent, accessible data explorer for the global burden.)
    • Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation. Global Burden of Disease: research overview and GBD Compare.
    • Climate and Clean Air Coalition: ccacoalition.org. (For the SLCP policy landscape.)
    • UNEP and WMO. "Integrated Assessment of Black Carbon and Tropospheric Ozone." 2011. The report that helped launch the CCAC.
