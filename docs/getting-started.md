# Part 5. Getting started: a suggested first few weeks

You do not need to do these in order, and we will shape them together, but here is a sensible on-ramp.

Week 1, orientation and people.
- Read Part 1 (the primer) and Part 2 (health impact assessment) of this guide. Skim the rest.
- Meet with me to set expectations and confirm your first task. Per the work plan, that first task is the concentration-response function literature review (see section 3.2.5), so part of week 1 is orienting to what a CRF is and what endpoints we need.
- Meet with Alexandra Koeck to walk the Monterrey Year 1 work plan and get access to the live project tracker, the shared Drive, and project channels.
- Start the "start here" readings: Southerland 2022 and Anenberg 2022 (our methods), and Fuller 2022 (the big picture).

Weeks 2 to 4, foundations and the CRF review.
- Work through the greenhouse gas and co-benefits readings (Shindell 2012; Anenberg 2012) so the climate-health link is solid, and the concentration-response readings (Burnett 2014 and 2018) since CRF selection is your first deliverable.
- Begin the CRF literature review in earnest: identify and document candidate Mexico-relevant functions for all-cause and cardiovascular mortality, cardiovascular emergency-department visits and hospitalizations, and respiratory hospitalizations.
- Get your R environment set up and start the geospatial basics (sf and terra), using Geocomputation with R as a guide. A good first exercise: load a satellite PM2.5 raster and a population grid, align them, and compute a population-weighted average for a region. I can hand you a worked example from the global cities project.
- Read the InMAP papers and browse the model site, so the Monterrey modeling will make sense even though UW runs it.
- Pull the PECC and PIGECA documents from the team and read the interventions, then look at our internal mapping of which interventions matter for black carbon and PM2.5.

Month 2 and beyond, into the work.
- Move from the CRF review into helping draft the HIA methodology document, then preliminary health benefit estimates, as the modeling outputs come online (see the sequence in 3.2.5).
- Sit in on stakeholder and partner meetings to absorb the project relationships and the bilingual, policy-facing style of the work.

A few working norms worth knowing early. We try to be precise rather than to oversell, especially on co-benefit claims and on any specific number. We never dress up an approximation as a precise figure; if a number is uncertain, we say so or mark it as a placeholder until it is verified. And in our written materials we use commas rather than dashes for asides, a small house-style preference you will see throughout.
