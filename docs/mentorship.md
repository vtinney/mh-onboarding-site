# Part 6. Mentorship, expectations, and how we will work together

This part is about the working relationship, which matters as much as the science for a good fellowship. Some of it is mine to set with you in our first conversations, so a few specifics are left as brackets to fill in together.

## 6.1 How we will work

- Regular one-on-ones [proposed cadence, for example weekly 30 to 60 minutes; we will set a standing time]. These are your time: progress, blockers, questions, and your own development, not just task updates.
- A simple rule for getting unstuck: try something first, and if you are still stuck after a reasonable effort (say, an afternoon on a technical problem), ask rather than spin. Being stuck is normal and asking early is a strength, not a failing. I would much rather hear about a blocker on Tuesday than at Friday's deadline.
- Default to over-communicating early. As you learn the project, flag assumptions and check direction before going deep, it saves rework.
- Use the right channel: quick things in chat, substantive discussion in our one-on-ones or a call, decisions and anything others need to see written down where the team can find it.

## 6.2 What I will provide

- Context and direction, so you understand not just the task but why it matters and how it fits.
- Regular, concrete feedback on your work, the kind you can act on, delivered directly and kindly.
- Advocacy for your development: connecting you with people, pointing you toward opportunities (conferences, co-authorship, skills), and making sure your growth is part of the plan.
- A reasonable, sustainable pace. This is a demanding, technical project and a learning curve is expected; I do not expect you to know it all on arrival, and I would rather you ramp well than burn out.

## 6.3 What I will expect from you

- Ownership of your deliverables: not perfection, but follow-through, and a heads-up when something is slipping.
- Good questions, asked early.
- Reproducible, documented work: organized code and data so that someone else (including future you) can rerun it. This is also a hard requirement of the project, since we archive to Zenodo and GitHub.
- Care with numbers and sources, in keeping with the house style: no fabricated or dressed-up figures, and clear marking of what is uncertain.
- Engagement with the team and the partners, including building toward bilingual comfort over time.

## 6.4 What success looks like

By the end of the fellowship, a strong outcome would include: having led the concentration-response and HIA-methodology components and grown into the full health impact assessment; meaningfully shaped the equity analysis; being a co-author on at least one peer-reviewed manuscript; having presented at an annual symposium and ideally a scientific conference; and having developed durable, portable skills in geospatial analysis, health impact assessment, and science-to-policy translation. ## 6.5 Feedback, reviews, and wellbeing

- Feedback runs both ways. I will give you regular feedback, and I want yours, on the project, on how our collaboration is working, and on this guide. Tell me what is unclear or missing.
- Formal checkpoints [any mid-point and end-of-fellowship reviews, and your supervisor of record for HR purposes].
- A note on wellbeing: sustainable pace matters, and so does asking for help, including outside work. If something in your workload or circumstances is making things hard, raise it with me early; we can adjust.

## 6.6 Authorship and credit

On the manuscripts, authorship follows standard scientific norms (for example the ICMJE criteria: substantial contribution to the work, involvement in drafting or revising, approval of the final version, and accountability). You are slated as a co-author on the first paper and a close contributor on the second. We will discuss authorship and contribution expectations openly and early for any output you work on, so credit is clear from the start rather than negotiated at the end.
